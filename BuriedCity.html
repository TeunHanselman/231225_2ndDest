In a world where every interaction is tense, and loading into any game comes with a significant risk of losing whatever you brought in, it feels strange to walk around with the sole purpose of documenting.

“I would honestly say I don’t notice all the little details, but if they’re absent, I notice how empty the world feels in general.”

“Fake” paintings are strewn across buildings to suggest that a life was lived. Are these paintings images retrieved from somewhere, or were they custom-made by an artist working on the game? What narrative do they suggest?

The act of noticing in a curated world is mostly an examination of the care those who made it put into it.

As I trace artefacts from the fallen world that came before, I start to feel like an archivist—the only one who cares to document the traces we have left of art and culture, while the rest (rightfully) focus on survival.

Unexpectedly, I start enjoying playing the game in this alternate way. Finding and documenting little details in confined spaces becomes a new game in and of itself.

Often, I find myself investigating objects that would, within the universe, have practical uses but, in the context of a game, don’t. For example, an electrical box placed in a building: in real life, its design would be determined by function, but in the game, it is crafted to suggest function, allowing our minds to fill in what it might once have been responsible for.
